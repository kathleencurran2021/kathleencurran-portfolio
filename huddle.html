Huddle: Designing an app to bring students together

Project Details

  • Role: UX Designer

Skills

  • Interaction Design
  • Semi-Structured Interviews
  • Wireframing
  • Prototyping
  • User Testing

Tools

  • Adobe XD
  • Miro

Overview

Research shows that peer-to-peer connections are vital to thriving in an academic environment. These connections drive success not only socially, but also in terms of information about the school and career connections. There is quite a bit of research on undergraduate social support and subsequent orientation programs, but these seem to be optional or not prioritized for graduate student programs.

In a program as large as the University of Michigan School of Information (UMSI), it is easy to get lost in the crowd, and it is especially difficult to enter this environment and connect with peers during Covid. This has resulted in a class that feels disconnected and lonely.

The Challenge

Huddle is a project that hopes to bring together graduate students at UMSI. While there were some online platforms like Facebook that some were using to communicate, there was no centralized platform that people were comfortable just connecting with each other on.

My project sought to answer the following:
  1. What kinds of connections at UMSI are students looking for?
  2. What has worked for students socially so far?
  3. Where are students struggling the most?
  4. How can a digital tool facilitate social connection at UMSI?

Scope and Constraints

This was a short term project, so I was not able to spend as much time doing testing and redesigning as I would have liked. This application is also limited to my audience of UMSI graduate students.

Process

Interviews

Before designing anything, I needed more information, so I conducted interviews with 5 UMSI students. Each interviewee expressed that they had difficulty making friends.

I had expected a result like this as it lined up with my initial research questions. However, most participants also expressed that before arriving in Ann Arbor, information had been difficult to find because there were no unified resources.

Any information they had found about Ann Arbor or UMSI had been disorganized or not specific to grad students, making it difficult to know if it was relevant to them.

Personas

I created two primary personas based off the motivations and behaviors I observed from my interviews. These personas were vital for building empathy during the design process.

Story Mapping

With my personas and scenarios constructed, I created a story map for each. After creating three individual maps, I examined the variation in tasks between the users created a common path story map on the similarities, using sticky notes so I could move tasks as needed.

With the story map, I was able to find patterns of use from my personas a that allowed me to begin development on a wireframe.

Early Prototyping

I began with a sketched wireframe, focusing more on tasks and interactions over aesthetics. My early designs were messy, as I focused on interactions and layout.

After getting feedback I created a mid-fidelity prototype. In this iteration, I'm thinking more about the details. I also added a navbar at the bottom for easier navigation.

User Testing, Feedback, and Finding Bugs

After creating the mid-fidelity prototype I engaged in user testing and got five participants from my user pool to test the app by clicking through the mid-fidelity prototype.

I gave each participant the same tasks and encouraged them to think aloud while they engaged with the prototype. At the end of each task, I recorded the ease of which they completed the task in a table.

The user testing showed that the prototype had inconsistent language that was confusing to users, as well as a bug in the navigation that made it impossible to complete one of the tasks.

Final Prototype

These iterations of prototyping and feedback allowed me to create an interactive hi-fidelity prototype. This prototype includes a forum for University of Michigan students to ask questions of each other and share information and an archive-style resource section where people can share and search for information organized under specific topics.

Conclusion

The final prototype addresses the user needs by helping students facilitate social connections both online and offline. The forum part of the prototype gives them a place to organize events, connect over common interests, or answer questions for each other. The resources library addresses the need for information by organizing information in one place and allowing people to contribute to it.

What I Learned

I learned a lot from this project. The first thing I learned was to stay open-minded and be ready to pivot. I was expecting to work on one problem, but my interviews ended up taking me a different direction, which surprised me, but it was a great lesson.

The second thing I learned was to trust the design process. When I began this project, it was tempting to try and start designing a solution immediately. But in going through the whole design process, I was able to ensure that I was solving the right problem, and confidently defend my design decisions when I presented this project at the end.

The last thing I learned was to seek feedback early and often. I really wanted my designs to be perfect early, and asking for feedback made me feel pretty vulnerable at first. But design is such an iterative process and getting another pair of eyes definitely helped me improve my design skills.
