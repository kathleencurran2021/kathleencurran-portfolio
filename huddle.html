Huddle: Designing an app to bring students together

Project Details

  • Time Frame: Fall 2021
  • Role: UX Designer

Skills

  • Interaction Design
  • Wireframing
  • Prototyping

Overview

Research shows that peer-to-peer connections are vital to thriving in an academic environment. These connections drive success not only socially, but also in terms of information about the school and career connections. There is quite a bit of research on undergraduate social support and subsequent orientation programs, but these seem to be optional or not prioritized for graduate student programs.

In a program as large as the University of Michigan School of Information (UMSI), it is easy to get lost in the crowd, and it is especially difficult to enter this environment and connect with peers during Covid. This has resulted in a class that feels disconnected and lonely.

The Challenge

Huddle is a project that hopes to bring together graduate students at UMSI. While there were some online platforms like Facebook that some were using to communicate, there was no centralized platform that people were comfortable just connecting with each other on.

My project sought to answer the following:
  1. What kinds of connections at UMSI are students looking for?
  2. What has worked for students socially so far?
  3. Where are students struggling the most?
  4. How can a digital tool facilitate social connection at UMSI?

Scope and Constraints

This was a short term project of about four months, so I was not able to spend as much time doing testing and redesigning as I would have liked. This application is also limited to my audience of UMSI graduate students.

Process

Interviews

Before designing anything, I needed more information, so I conducted interviews with 5 UMSI students. Each interviewee expressed that they had difficulty making friends.

I had expected a result like this as it lined up with my initial research questions. However, most participants also expressed that wanted a grad student-specific guide to UMSI.

Any other information they had found about Ann Arbor or UMSI had been disorganized or not specific to grad students, making it difficult to know if it was relevant to them.

Personas

I created two primary personas based off the motivations and behaviors I observed from my interviews. These personas were vital for building empathy during the design process.

Survey

To better understand how users of varying abilities engaged with sahiyo.org, we launched a survey to the Sahiyo community, asking about their experience with the website, their role in Sahiyo, their disability status, and how often they used the site.

The survey had several limitations that weakened our data’s accuracy. First, we did not receive enough responses for our survey results to be statistically significant. Secondly, the majority of respondents were either Sahiyo staff or volunteers which may not be representative of typical users.

However, our results indicated that users with disabilities had a more difficult time using the site than those without disabilities.

While the survey gathered general data about users’ experiences on the website, it did not provide any details behind why users felt the way they did. Because of this, we decided to perform a heuristic evaluation to find specific instances of usability issues, and usability testing to get further insight on our survey responses.

Heuristic Evaluation

Because of our results from the survey, we wanted to determine the specific accessibility issues with sahiyo.org. Each team member examined the website according to Nielsen's usability heuristics. We prioritized each issue by severity level.

Most of the issues we found were related to the visual design of the site. We recommended that Sahiyo implement several changes to the website, such as increasing the color contrast of fonts and buttons, adding alternative text to images, increasing keyboard accessibility, and adding text decoration to links to make them more distinct.

Usability Testing

In our usability tests, we wanted to assess users' ability to find information on sahiyo.org, specifically those who have visual impairments such as low vision or colorblindness.

We used word of mouth, online forums, and survey responses to recruit users with visual disabilities and/or Sahiyo connections. We recruited five individuals of varying ages, disabilities, and levels of experience with sahiyo.org.

Tests were conducted via Zoom and ranged from 20 – 60 minutes. View Protocol Here Users generally had high success with general navigation within the site. Users experienced the most difficulty locating an event in the calendar which had the lowest average satisfaction rate at 1.6 and the lowest success rate at 50%. Additionally, some aspects of the site were out of date or broken, leaving users frustrated.

For each task, the we recorded the participant's completion of the task, the time they took, and how satisfying they found the task. We also kept observations of participants in a color-coded spreadsheet, which allowed us to keep track of common issues and comments that users encountered.

Recommendations

Our recommendations seek to improve the accessibility of sahiyo.org in terms of online accessibility guidelines, as well as creating a site where information and community is easily available for those who are looking for it.

We recommend...

  1. Improving site navigation by reorganizing the menu to align more with user expectations, and fixing broken links.
  2. Ensuring a more consistent site structure by removing outdated content and validate page titles to make sure they accurately reflect page content.
  3. Automatically redirecting old site to the new one.
  4. Increasing accessibility by fixing contrast issues with links, adding alt-text to all non-textual content, and ensuring proper responsiveness.
Final Stakeholder Presentation

Conclusion

Next Steps

While our suggestions will help increase accessibility on sahiyo.org, we recommend that the Sahiyo team has regular audits of their content to make sure everything is up to date and still accessible to the community.

We also recommend further accessibility testing to the mobile site, since we were unable to include that in our user testing.

However, making even small changes to increase sahiyo.org’s accessibility will increase community engagement by making it easier for users to find information and interact with the site content.

What I Learned

While I'm someone who loves to try to plan for every eventuality, there’s still so much value to be gained from studies that don’t go perfectly, and being flexible is so important for being able to see that potential.

My team learned this with our survey study. While we had few participants, their answers about their experiences (especially those who reported they had disabilities) pointed us towards focusing more on people with disabilities in our usability testing. We were able to identify some areas for improvement that hadn't been on our radar before.
