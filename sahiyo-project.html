Overview

Sahiyo (pronounced Say-yo) is an international organization dedicated to fighting for women's rights, specifically in the area of female genital cutting (FGC). They work to empower communities to end FGC and create social change through education and community involvement on their website, sahiyo.org.

The Challenge

Sahiyo uses their website to post resources for FGC as well as foster community involvement. However, the website is not always accessible and users often have difficulty finding the resources they need, particularly with event information. Throughout the study, our team sought to answer the following question:

“What accessibility challenges do users encounter when they use Sahiyo’s website, and how are those issues negatively impacting user engagement across the site?”

Scope and Constraints

The project was limited to four months. Due to this time constraint, we decided to work primarily with the desktop version of sahiyo.org because analytics showed that most visitors accessed the site on desktop, despite there being a mobile version as well.

Process

Interview Study

We conducted an interview study which sought to understand the primary goals, pain points, and needs of users navigating the Sahiyo website.

We conducted six interviews each lasting between 30-60 minutes. Interviewees were recruited with the help of Sahiyo leadership and through online forums for people with disabilities.

Key Findings:

Personas

We used information from our interviews to create personas for sahiyo.org users. These personas allowed us to better understand our target audience and keep in mind user's needs as we made decisions.

Survey

To better understand how users of varying abilities engaged with sahiyo.org, we launched a survey to the Sahiyo community. Our goal was to understand people's experiences with the site, especially for anyone who had a disability.

The survey had several limitations. First, we did not receive enough responses for our survey results to be statistically significant. Secondly, the majority of respondents were either Sahiyo staff or volunteers which may not be representative of typical users.

However, our results indicated that users with disabilities had a more negative experience using the site than those without disabilities.

The survey showed that people with reported disabilities had lower satisfaction with the site in every area. In particular, their experiences in finding information on Sahiyo events was significantly more negative.

These responses suggested that there were issues within the site affecting accessibility and engagement, especially when it came to Sahiyo events.

Because of this, we decided to perform a heuristic evaluation to identify specific usability issues.

Heuristic Evaluation

Because of our results from the survey, we wanted to determine the specific accessibility issues with sahiyo.org. Each team member examined the website according to Nielsen's usability heuristics . We prioritized each issue by severity level.

Most of the issues we found were related to the visual design of the site. We recommended that Sahiyo implement several changes to the website, such as increasing the color contrast of fonts and buttons, adding alternative text to images, increasing keyboard accessibility, and adding text decoration to links to make them more distinct.

Usability Testing

In our usability tests, we wanted to assess users' ability to find information on sahiyo.org, specifically those who have visual impairments such as low vision or colorblindness.

We used word of mouth, online forums, and survey responses to recruit users with visual disabilities and/or Sahiyo connections. We recruited five individuals of varying ages, disabilities, and levels of experience with sahiyo.org.

Tests were conducted via Zoom and ranged from 20 – 60 minutes. View Protocol Here Users generally had high success with general navigation within the site and had the most frustration locating an event in the calendar. Additionally, some aspects of the site were out of date or broken, leaving users frustrated.

For each task, the we recorded the participant's completion of the task, the time they took, and how satisfying they found the task. We also kept observations of participants in a color-coded spreadsheet, which allowed us to keep track of common issues and comments that users encountered.

Recommendations

Our recommendations seek to improve the accessibility of sahiyo.org in terms of online accessibility guidelines, as well as creating a site where information and community is easily available for those who are looking for it.

We recommend...

  1. Improving site navigation by reorganizing the menu to align more with user expectations, and fixing broken links.
  2. Ensuring a more consistent site structure by removing outdated content and validate page titles to make sure they accurately reflect page content.
  3. Automatically redirecting old site to the new one.
  4. Increasing accessibility by fixing contrast issues with links, adding alt-text to all non-textual content, and ensuring proper responsiveness.

Conclusion

Next Steps

While our suggestions will help increase accessibility on sahiyo.org, we recommend that the Sahiyo team has regular audits of their content to make sure everything is up to date and still accessible to the community.

We also recommend further accessibility testing to the mobile site, since we were unable to include that in our user testing.

However, making even small changes to increase sahiyo.org’s accessibility will increase community engagement by making it easier for users to find information and interact with the site content.

What I Learned

While I love to try to plan for every eventuality, there’s still so much value to be gained from studies that don’t go perfectly, and being flexible is so important for being able to see that potential.

My team learned this with our survey study. While we had few participants, their answers about their experiences (especially those who reported they had disabilities) pointed us towards focusing more on people with disabilities in our usability testing. We were able to identify some areas for improvement that hadn't been on our radar before.
