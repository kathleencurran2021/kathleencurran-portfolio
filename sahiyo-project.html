Building Community through Improved Accessibility

  • Role: UX Researcher
  • Client: Sahiyo
  • Skills: Usability Testing, User Interviews, Heuristic Evaluation, Survey Design
  • Tools: Figma, Qualtrics, Google Analytics
  • Team: Grace Brindle, Alyssa Downs, Austin Williams
  • Duration: January - April 2022

Problem Sahiyo knew they had some accessibility issues with their site, but weren’t sure of the extent to which it was excluding people, or what to prioritize to increase engagement with their community.

Solution
We created a guide of recommendations for prioritizing accessibility, as well as tools they can use to further monitor their accessibility. This solution should increase accessibility among visually impaired users, and increase the site's overall usability.

Overview

Sahiyo (pronounced Say-yo) is an international organization dedicated to fighting for women's rights, specifically in the area of female genital cutting (FGC). They work to empower communities to end FGC and create social change through education and community involvement on their website, sahiyo.org.

The Challenge

Sahiyo uses their website to share resources for FGC and create community. However, the website is not completelt accessible and users often have difficulty finding the resources they need. Throughout the study, our team sought to answer the following question:

“What accessibility challenges do users encounter when they use Sahiyo’s website, and how do those issues impact user engagement across the site?”

Scope and Constraints

The project was limited to four months. Analytics showed that visitors primarily accessed the site on the desktop version so we focused our efforts there.

Process

Understanding User Needs

We conducted an interview study which sought to understand the primary goals, pain points, and needs of users navigating the Sahiyo website.

We conducted six interviews each lasting between 30-60 minutes. Interviewees were recruited with the help of Sahiyo leadership and through online forums for people with disabilities.

Key Findings:

1

Users struggle with accessibility issues such as responsiveness when searching for resources

2

Storytelling is vital to the Sahiyo community, but the blog is difficult to navigate

3

Users often accidentally navigated to Sahiyo’s old website, which is out of date

4

Sahiyo.org is effective at helping users find resources about FGC

Survey

To better understand how users of varying abilities engaged with sahiyo.org, we launched a survey to the Sahiyo community. Our goal was to understand people's experiences with the site, especially for anyone with a disability.

The survey had several limitations. First, we did not receive enough responses for our survey results to be statistically significant. Secondly, the majority of respondents were either Sahiyo staff or volunteers which may not be representative of typical users.

However, our results indicated that users with disabilities had a more negative experience using the site than those without disabilities.

The survey showed that people who reported disabilities had lower satisfaction with the site in every area. In particular, their experiences in finding information on Sahiyo events was significantly more negative.

These responses suggested that there were issues within the site affecting accessibility and engagement , especially when it came to Sahiyo events.

Because of this, we decided to perform a heuristic evaluation to identify specific usability issues.

Identifying Accessibility Issues

Because of our results from the survey, we wanted to determine the specific accessibility issues with sahiyo.org. Each team member examined the website according to Nielsen's usability heuristics. We prioritized each issue by severity level.

Most of the issues we found were related to the visual design of the site. We recommended that Sahiyo implement several changes, such as:

  • increasing the color contrast of fonts and buttons
  • adding alternative text to images
  • increasing keyboard accessibility
  • adding text decoration to links to make them more distinct

Usability Testing

In our usability tests, we assessed sahiyo.org and how well users with visual impairments such as low vision or colorblindness were able to engage with the site.

Tests were conducted via Zoom and ranged from 20 – 60 minutes. View Protocol Here For each task, the we recorded the participant's completion of the task, the time they took, and how satisfying they found the task. We also kept observations of participants in a color-coded spreadsheet, which allowed us to keep track of common issues and comments that users encountered.

Users generally had high success with general navigation within the site. They were generally able to locate Sahiyo's blog, which is consistent with the satisfaction results from our survey.

However, users had difficulty locating Sahiyo events in the calendar. Additionally, some resources on the site were out of date or broken, leaving users frustrated.

Recommendations

Our recommendations seek to improve the accessibility of sahiyo.org in terms of online accessibility guidelines, as well as creating a site where information and community is easily available for those who are looking for it.

We recommend...

Reorganize Navigation

Some aspects of site navigation were confusing to users. Sahiyo should reorganize their top menu to align more with user expectations and fix broken links.

Ensure Content is Up-To-Date

Sahiyo should remove outdated content from search results and validate page titles to make sure they accurately reflect page content.

Redirect Old Site to Current Site

People often accidentally navigate to Sahiyo's old site. Sahiyo should set up the old site to redirect all users to the new site to avoid confusion.

Fix Web Accessibility Issues

Sahiyo should increase accessibility by fixing color contrast issues with links, adding alt-text to all non-textual content, and ensuring proper responsiveness.

Final Stakeholder Presentation

Conclusion

Next Steps

During our research, we discovered that while many of the accessibility issues on sahiyo.org were things that were easily fixable (color contrast, alt-text, responsiveness, etc) they caused much frustration for our users and negatively impacted how they used the site.

While we recommended specific ways to fix the accessibility issues we found, it will be important for Sahiyo to conduct regular audits of their site to make sure everything is up to date and still accessible to the community. We also recommend further accessibility testing to the mobile site, since we were unable to include that in our user testing.

Making even small changes to increase sahiyo.org’s accessibility will increase community engagement by making it easier for users to find information and interact with the site content, helping them continue their mission of creating positive social change and ending FGC.

What I Learned

If there had been more time, I would have loved to conduct a card sort exercise. We were able to come up with solutions for many of the accessibility issues, but our usability test showed there was confusion with sahiyo.org's organization, and our users had difficulty finding information. A card sort could help determine a better way to structure the site.

I also learned there’s still so much value to gain from studies that don’t go perfectly . Our survey didn't get the number of responses responses we had hoped, but the responses we did receive helped the team find the right direction for usability testing. Being flexible and willing to pivot helped the team see value in the data we got, and ultimately find success.
